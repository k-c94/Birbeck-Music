What is a Music Therapist?

Trained professionals who use music to support health, well-being, and quality of life.

A music therapist is a board-certified healthcare professional who uses music purposefully within a therapeutic relationship to support development, health, and well-being. Music therapists are not just musicians — they are trained clinicians who assess each client's needs and design evidence-based interventions using music.

🎵

Education & Training

Music therapists complete an accredited university degree in music therapy, including coursework in psychology, neuroscience, music theory, and clinical practice. They then complete a supervised internship before sitting a board certification exam.

🧠

How They Work

Sessions may involve listening to music, playing instruments, songwriting, improvisation, or movement to music. The approach is tailored to each individual's goals, whether those are emotional, cognitive, social, or physical in nature.

💕

Who They Help

Music therapists work with people of all ages and backgrounds — from children with developmental challenges to adults managing anxiety, depression, or chronic pain, and elderly individuals living with dementia or Alzheimer's disease.

🌱

Evidence-Based Practice

Music therapy is grounded in research. Therapists draw on clinical evidence to select interventions that are appropriate and effective, continuously evaluating progress and adapting treatment plans to meet each client's evolving needs.

Music Therapist vs. Music Teacher

Music Therapist

  • Focuses on therapeutic goals
  • Works in clinical or healthcare settings
  • Assesses and evaluates client progress
  • Board-certified healthcare professional
  • Uses music as a therapeutic tool

Music Teacher

  • Focuses on musical skill development
  • Works in educational settings
  • Teaches theory, technique, and repertoire
  • Certified educator
  • Uses music as an artistic goal
